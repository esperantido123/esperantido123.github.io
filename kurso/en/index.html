Letters

Each letter is pronounced and always in the same way. The letters Q, W, X and Y are not part of the alphabet. There are 6 additional letters: Ĉ, Ĝ, Ĥ, Ĵ, Ŝ and Ŭ. At lernu.net/gramatiko/skribo you can hear the pronunciation by clicking the letters.

When a word has two or more vowels, the stress is always at the second to last vowel. Because Ŭ is no vowel, you say for example:

  • Not aŬto but Aŭto (the vowels are AO, so the A is pronounced more strongly)
  • Not apenAŭ but apEnaŭ (the vowels are AEA, so the E is pronounced more strongly)

Nowadays it's easy to type the letters Ĉ, Ĝ, Ĥ, Ĵ, Ŝ and Ŭ on a computer or smartphone. On smartphones you may just have to check the Esperanto keyboard in your settings - after that, long-pressing "s" displays the option "ŝ". After installing a small program on your computer you can type "sx" to get "ŝ" on your screen.

Word order

The basic word order in Esperantido 123 is:

subject - verb (- direct object)
Mi amas vi. I love you.
Vi amas mi. You love me.
Ŝi havas blua piedpilko. She has a blue football.
La piedpilko, kiu havas blua koloro, estas ŝia. The football that has a blue color is hers.
Internaciaj lingvoj interesas mi. International languages interest me.
Ŝi kantas. She sings.

You can see that words don't change depending on their "role" in a sentence. In English, "I" can change to "me".


If you want to put the sentence "ŝi havas blua piedpilko" (she has a blue football) in a different word order, your can choose "Blua piedpilko ŝi havas". The second allowed word order is:

direct object - subject - verb
Blua piedpilko ŝi havas! She has a blue football!
Vi devas redoni ĉiuj aferoj, kiuj vi prenis. You must return all the things (that) you took.

This word order is often necessary to refer back to what was said before the comma.


In some cases you may want to make something clear about the subject of the sentence via a subordinate clause. Then you can put the subject at the end, followed by a comma, so that you can refer back to it:

verb - direct object - subject
Interesas mi la lingvoj, kiuj estas facile lerneblaj. I am interested in languages that are easy to learn.

The above rules are about sentences that have a subject. The rules below cover the rest of the possibilities:

imperative form - direct object
Uzu tio! Use that!

infinitive - direct object
Lerni Esperantido 123 kostas malmulte da tempo. Learning Esperanto costs little time.

adverbial participle - direct object
Manĝante pomo ŝi legis sia libro. While eating an apple she read her book.

The fact that Esperantido 123 prescribes word orders is its biggest difference from Esperanto. In Esperanto, an "-n" is placed at the end of the direct object (and that letter is also used for table words and adjectives):

Esperantido 123 Esperanto
Mi amas vi. Mi amas vin.
Vi amas mi. Vi amas min.
Ŝi havas blua piedpilko. Ŝi havas bluan piedpilkon.
La piedpilko, kiu havas blua koloro, estas ŝia. La piedpilko, kiu havas bluan koloron, estas ŝia.
Internaciaj lingvoj interesas mi. Internaciaj lingvoj interesas min.
Ŝi kantas. Ŝi kantas.
Interesas mi la lingvoj, kiuj estas facile lerneblaj. Interesas min la lingvoj, kiuj estas facile lerneblaj.
Manĝante pomo hi legis sia libro. Manĝante pomon li legis sian libron.
Eble uzi vortordo estas pli facile ol uzi la akuzativo. Eble uzi vortordon estas pli facile ol uzi la akuzativon.

During this course you will learn that the N ending in Esperanto is used for more things than the direct object. For reading Esperanto or listening to others, you will need to know the other cases of using N. Although it is not necessary for Esperantido 123, we will cover it, because you will most likely read more in Esperanto than in Esperantido 123. If you use the language Esperantido 123 on the internet (publicly visible), in writing or in a YouTube video, you contribute to the spread of the language. The same applies to the use of Esperanto. The more people use it (for example in reviews of online purchases via an international website), the better known it will become and the more people will learn about it.

Suffixes

Below the heading "Nouns" we saw that patro = parent, patriĉo = father, patrino = mother. The suffixes IĈ/IN make a neutral word masculine/feminine.

At lernu.net/gramatiko/sufiksoj you will see all the suffixes that apply in Esperanto and Esperantido 123.

The suffix IĈ is not (yet) widely used in Esperanto (and it will most likely remain that way). In Esperanto, the word "patro" only means "father". However, Esperanto does have a solution to this problem: the prefix "GE" means "both genders". The Esperanto word "gepatroj" therefore equals the Esperantido 123 word "patroj". The "ge-" prefix is less often used for words in the singular form "gepatro" and it is never used for professions. Nowadays it is a safe assumption that the simple "-o" form in professions is neutral, such as "tradukisto" (translator, male or female). With the word "bovo" it is not clear whether it concerns a male (bull) or whether it can be both (bovo = bull or cow). To emphasize that it is a bull, you can use the word "viro" (= man) in Esperanto and you can say "virbovo". To express both genders in writing, you can also write "kantist(in)o" with brackets. (In Esperantido 123 this is not needed.)

Two common suffixes are "igi" (to do) and "iĝi" (to become):

Examples Translation
bluigi (blua = (the color) blue) to make blue, to let something become blue
bluiĝi become blue
klarigi (klara = clear) make clear, to explain
devigi (devi = to have to) to force
paliĝi (pala = pale) to pale

Prefixes

At lernu.net/gramatiko/prefiksoj you can find information about prefixes. The most notable prefix is MAL.

Examples: "malfeliĉa" = "unhappy", "malsana" = "unhealthy".

This prefix in itself is not that remarkable in meaning, as there are many national languages that have such a prefix. What is special is that it is used so often. Originally there was not even a word for "left", because people only used "maldekstra". (The word "dekstra" means "right".)

For almost all "mal" words there is an alternative now. For example:

  • "aperta" = "malfermita" (open, the opposite of closed)
  • "liva" = "maldekstra" (left, the opposite of right)
However, most people continue to use the "mal" words, for example because they believe that an international language should be easy to learn (and less words means less to study). The use of "mal" words is very accepted.

When you drive together, you should of course choose what works best for the driver. Does the driver want you to emphasize the A in MAL ("mAldekstra") instead of saying "maldEkstra"? Do that. Does the driver want you to use "liva"? Do that. Of course, hand gestures are also an option.

Direction

Esperanto also uses the N-ending to indicate that something is moving toward... for example, "home": "Mi iras hejmen." (= "I'm going home.")

In Esperantido 123 it was decided to avoid all N-endings, so we use the Esperanto word "al" (= to): "Mi iras al-hejme.". Here "al-hejme" is an adverb and says something about the verb "iri". You could also say "Mi iras al hejmo" or "Mi iras al mia hejmo".

Be careful when driving if someone speaks Esperanto and you speak Esperantido 123...:
“Iru al-dekstre!” (= "Turn right!") sounds very similar to "Iru maldekstren!" (= "Turn left!").
Perhaps it is better not to use the word "al-": "Dekstre!".

Esperanto uses the word "supren" for "up" and "malsupren" for "down". In Espernatido 123 you would then use "al-supre" and "al-malsupre". The literal meaning of "al-supre"/"supren" is "towards the top", because "supro" means "top". If you find that disturbing, there is an alternative: the Esperanto dictionary reta-vortaro.de also contains the word "sor" as a synonym for "supren", so you could use that too.

Examples Translation
La hundo enkuris nia domo. The dog ran into our house.
La hundo kuris al en nia domo. The dog ran into our house.
La hundo kuris en nia domo. The dog ran in our house.
Li eliris. = Li iris al-ekstere. He/she went outside.
Al-kie vi iras? Where are you going?

Note that there is a space between "al" and the preposition: al antaŭ, al malantaŭ, al apud, al ĉe, al ĉirkaŭ, al ekster, al en, al inter, al post, al sub, al super, al sur, al trans, etc.

With adverbs, the proposal in Esperantido 123 is to use a dash: not "al hejme" but "al-hejme".

Tenses

How participles can be used is described in detail at lernu.net/gramatiko/participoj.

Make sure that you know how to form words like "lernanto" and "mangante".

Esperanto speakers and writers usually use the simplest tense, for example "Mi legis tiu libro!" ("I read that book!") instead of "Mi estis leginta tiu libro!" ("I read that book!").

Instead of "Hi estis naskita en Januaro" (= "He was born in January") people often say "Hi naskiĝis en Januaro" - making use of the suffix "IĜ" (see lernu.net/gramatiko/sufiksoj).

You will often encounter the use of "-iĝis". In addition, some authors also use "-iĝas", for example in "uziĝas", while "estas uzata" (= "is used") would be more logical, because there isn't really a "transition to a new state, to a new place, to a new action".

Akuzativo

As discussed earlier, the direct object in Esperanto gets a final letter N, and the N is also used to indicate a movement in the direction of an object. There are still a few cases where an N is added, including "measurement" and "point in time".

More information about using N can be found at lernu.net/gramatiko/akuzativo.

The fact that Esperantido 123 uses word orders when there is a direct object, makes the language less attractive to poets. They will probably continue to use Esperanto.

Hi?

People who are already familiar with Esperanto may also have doubts about the introduction of the personal pronoun "hi" and may have the following question: Why would you use li, hi, ŝi when you can also use ri, li, ŝi? A counter question could be: If "ri" is not yet fully accepted in Esperanto, would one succeed to "enforce" it in Esperantido 123? Probably not; probably Esperantido 123 speakers would (also?) feel bad about using "ri": you don't want to feel like you're "campaigning" during normal conversations.

In several books, "li" is used as a neutral form; that also makes it more attractive to say: Let's make "li" the neutral form! Everyone is already used to "li", so using it will come naturally! Then, because many Esperanto speakers are familiar with the English language, “hi” will also be easy to pick up. If it feels strange at first to express so explicitly that a person is male, remind yourself that you should feel the same about expressing femininity. In other words, if you prefer not to use "hi" and "-iĉo", then you should feel and act the same regarding "ŝi" and "-ino".
